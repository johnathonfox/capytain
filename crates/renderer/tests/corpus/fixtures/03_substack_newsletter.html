On software lasting

By A. Reader · Mar 14, 2026

A friend asked me recently what makes a piece of software "last." Not in the ordinary sense of continuing to run — most code runs until someone unplugs it — but in the sense of continuing to matter ten, twenty, forty years on.

I think the obvious answer is correct: the tools that last are the ones people keep reaching for. But the interesting question is why people keep reaching for them. And the answer, when I look at the tools I reach for most, is almost always the same.

"The tools I keep reaching for are the ones that get out of my way."

Unix text-processing. SQLite. Rust's borrow checker, now that I've had enough time with it. Each one, after some initial friction, disappears into the work. You stop thinking about the tool and start thinking about the problem.

The counterexample is equally instructive. I've retired more IDE plugins, chat clients, and "productivity suites" than I can count. They had features. They had polish. But they insisted on being there — reminding me of themselves — and eventually I found my way to a simpler tool that didn't.
